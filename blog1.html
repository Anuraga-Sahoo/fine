Futures trading on exchanges like NCDEX isn’t just for financial pros—it’s a practical tool for farmers, traders, and businesses to manage price risks. If you're curious about how India's agricultural futures market works, this guide breaks it down in plain English with real-world examples and key concepts.

What is a Futures Contract on NCDEX?

A futures contract on the National Commodity and Derivatives Exchange (NCDEX) is a legally binding agreement to buy or sell a specific agricultural commodity—like wheat, jeera, or chana—at a fixed price on a set future date.

Unlike informal deals, NCDEX futures are:

  • Standardized (in terms of quantity, quality, and delivery)
  • Exchange-traded (providing transparency and safety)
  • Backed by a clearing corporation (removing counterparty risk)

    • Real Example:

    A food company wants to buy 100 kg of wheat in April. In January, they lock in a price of ₹25/kg on NCDEX. Whether the market price later goes up to ₹30 or down to ₹22, they still buy it at ₹25—protecting them from price swings.

    Key Terms in NCDEX Futures Trading

    1. Underlying Asset

    The commodity you're trading. Example: Wheat in a wheat futures contract.

    2. Lot Size

    The standardized quantity in a single contract. For wheat: 10 quintals.

    3. Tick Size

    The minimum price movement allowed. If tick size is ₹1, price moves can be ₹2,000 → ₹2,001, but not ₹2,000.50.

    4. Contract Cycle

    • Near Month: Current
    • Next Month: Following
    • Month: Later months

    5. Expiry Date

    Last day of trading for the contract.

    6. Settlement Types

    • Cash Settlement: Profit/loss in cash
    • Physical Delivery: Actual commodity is exchanged

    7. Mark-to-Market (MTM)

    Daily profit/loss adjustments based on market prices.

    8. Initial Margin

    Security deposit to start trading (e.g., ₹10,000 for a ₹2L contract).

    9. Maintenance Margin

    Minimum balance to keep your position open—replenish if it drops.

    10. Open Interest

    Total number of open, unsettled contracts—used to gauge market activity.

    11. Volume

    Number of contracts traded daily—shows market interest.

    12. Delivery Centre

    Warehouse designated for commodity delivery (e.g., in Punjab for wheat).

    Long Position: How It Works

    Taking a long position means buying a futures contract—profiting if market prices rise above the agreed price.

    Example:

    • Futures Buy Price: ₹2,000
    • Spot Price at Expiry: ₹2,200
    • Profit: ₹200/quintal × 10 quintals = ₹2,000

    Understanding Cost-of-Carry

    Cost-of-Carry = Extra costs incurred to hold a commodity until delivery.

    Includes:

    • Storage (e.g., warehousing)
    • Insurance
    • Financing/Interest
    • Transportation
    • Preservation (for perishable goods)

    Formula:

    Futures Price = Spot Price + Cost-of-Carry

    Example:

    • Spot Price: ₹8,000
    • September Futures Price: ₹8,340
    • Carry Costs: ₹340 → justifies the higher futures price

    Price Patterns: Convergence, Contango, and Backwardation

    Convergence:

    As contract expiry approaches, the futures and spot prices merge.

    Contango (Rising Market):

    • Futures Price > Spot Price
    • Signals rising price expectation + storage/interest costs

    Backwardation (Falling Market):

    • Futures Price < Spot Price
    • Signals expected fall in price (e.g., during harvest season)

    Calculating Fair Value of Futures

    Fair Value= Spot Price + Cost-of-Carry

    Example:

    • Spot Wheat Price: ₹2,500
    • Interest + Storage Cost (90 days): ₹43.14
    • Fair Value = ₹2,543.14

    If actual futures price = ₹2,560 → Overpriced → Sell
    If it's ₹2,530 → Underpriced → Buy

    Practical Example: Wheat Futures on NCDEX

    • Spot Price: ₹2,500
    • Futures Price: ₹2,540
    • Contract Size: 10MT = 100 quintals
    • Margin Requirement: 5% = ₹12,700

    Scenario A: Price drops to ₹2,450

    • Futures Loss = ₹9,000
    • Spot Gain = ₹9,000
    • Net Effect = Neutral

    Scenario B: Price rises to ₹2,600

    • Futures Gain = ₹6,000
    • Spot Cost ↑ ₹6,000
    • Net Effect = Neutral

    Final Thoughts: Why NCDEX Futures Matter

    Whether you’re a farmer hedging against price drops, a food company locking in input costs, or a trader speculating on seasonal trends—NCDEX futures offer a structured, transparent, and low-risk way to engage in India’s agri commodity markets.

    Futures contracts not only provide price stability but also support smarter decision-making in a market where uncertainty is the only constant.
