Futures trading isn’t just for Wall Street professionals. Whether you’re an ambitious retail investor or a risk-averse corporation, futures contracts can be powerful tools—either to chase profits or shield against market swings. In this article, we’ll break down two core futures trading strategies: speculation and hedging.

Speculation with Futures

What Drives Futures Prices?

Before diving into strategies, it’s crucial to understand what influences futures prices:

  • Underlying Asset Price: Futures mirror the price of the asset they represent (gold, oil, indices, etc.).
  • Cost-of-Carry: Includes expenses like storage, insurance, and interest.
  • Market Expectations: Sentiment, trends, and analysis shape price predictions.
  • Economic Indicators: Interest rates, inflation, and government policies affect futures.
  • Supply & Demand: Natural events, geopolitics, and production shifts can cause volatility.
  • Arbitrage: Helps align futures with spot prices as expiration nears.
  • Liquidity: Popular contracts (e.g., Nifty, crude oil) ensure smoother trade execution.

How Traders Speculate Using Futures

Speculators bet on price movements to earn profits. They often use leverage, meaning they control large positions with a small upfront margin (typically 5–15% of the contract value). This can supercharge gains—but also magnify losses. Hence, disciplined risk management is non-negotiable.

Example 1: Going Long (Buying Futures)

  • A trader buys 1 gold futures contract (1kg) at ₹62,000 per 10gm.
  • If gold rises to ₹65,000 per 10gm:
  • Profit = (₹65,000 – ₹62,000) × 100 = ₹3,00,000

Example 2: Going Short (Selling Futures)

  • A trader sells 1 Nifty futures contract at ₹22,000.
  • If Nifty falls to ₹21,000:
  • Profit = (₹22,000 – ₹21,000) × 50 (lot size) = ₹50,000

Common Futures Trading Strategies

Directional Bets:

  • Go long if expecting a rise, go short if expecting a fall.

Spread Trading:

  • Calendar Spread: Buy and sell the same asset with different expiry dates.
  • Inter-Market Spread: Trade price differences between related markets.

Momentum-Based:

  • Trend Following: Ride a continuing price trend.
  • Breakout Trading: Enter when price breaches key support/resistance levels.

Event-Based Trading:

  • React to earnings reports, economic data, or policy announcements.

Pair Trading:

  • Go long on one asset and short on a correlated asset.

Scalping & Intraday:

  • Use high liquidity to profit from small price moves in a single day.

Arbitrage:

  • Exploit price differences between spot and futures for low-risk gains.

Quick Formula for Profit/Loss:

P/L = (Exit Price – Entry Price) × Lot Size × No. of Contracts

Hedging with Futures

What is Hedging?

Hedging is the art of reducing risk. Unlike speculation, the goal isn’t to profit—it’s to protect. By taking an opposite position in a futures contract, companies and investors can insulate themselves from adverse price movements.

Why Hedge?

  • Guard Against Volatility: Markets are inherently unpredictable.
  • Ensure Cost Predictability: Businesses lock in raw material costs or selling prices.
  • Stabilize Investments: Investors shield portfolios during turbulent times.

How Hedging Works

For Companies:

  • Fix Costs: A food company buys wheat futures to lock raw material prices.
  • Secure Revenues: An oil producer sells oil futures to guarantee future selling prices.

For Investors:

  • Protect Portfolios: Sell stock index futures to offset falling equity prices.
  • Manage FX Risk: Use currency futures to fix exchange rates and reduce losses.

Hedging in Action

Example 1: Hedging Oil Prices

  • An oil company expects to buy 10,000 barrels in 2 months at ₹6,000/barrel.
  • They buy futures at ₹6,000 to hedge.

If prices rise to ₹6,500:

  • Pay ₹6,500 in the market
  • Gain ₹500 on futures
  • Net cost = ₹6,000/barrel

If prices fall to ₹5,500:

  • Pay ₹5,500 in the market
  • Lose ₹500 on futures
  • Net cost = ₹6,000/barrel

Example 2: Hedging a Stock Portfolio

  • An investor owns ₹20 lakh in Infosys shares.
  • Fearing a downturn, they sell Nifty futures.

If Nifty drops 10%:

  • Stock value falls by ₹2 lakh
  • Futures gain ₹2 lakh
  • Net effect = no loss

If Nifty rises 10%:

  • Stock gains ₹2 lakh
  • Futures lose ₹2 lakh
  • Net effect = no gain, but portfolio stays protected

Key Takeaways: Why Use Futures?

  • Enables profit from market direction with limited capital.
  • Offers tools to protect against losses in volatile environments.
  • Helps businesses stabilize costs and revenues.
  • Ideal for both active traders and long-term investors when used wisely.

Futures trading isn’t a one-size-fits-all game. Speculation can offer high rewards but comes with risk. Hedging, on the other hand, provides safety and stability. Master both, and you’ll have a well-rounded toolkit for navigating modern financial markets.
